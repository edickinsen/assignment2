Reshma Saujani

Rehmsa Saujani has always liked a challenge. The Illinois native and daughter of Indian refugees understood the impact education could have on her future. She graduated from Harvard Law School and spent several years working as an attorney in a private firm. Feeling unfulfilled professionally, Saujani became the first South Asian woman to run for Congress in 2010. Despite losing, the election marked a turning point for Saujani. She left the private sector and started the international nonprofit organization Girls Who Code.

Girls Who Code

Reshma Saujani is the founder and CEO of Girls Who Code. The organization's goal is to close the gap in who creates technology and shift the image of what a computer programmer looks like to include women and people of color. The organization organizes an afterschool club for girls in grades 3-12 to introduce coding. They also host a two week summer program for high school girls. Additionally, they implement college programs to connect students and alumni with other women in technology.

The results are telling. Gils Who Code has 8,500 clubs worldwide and has reached over 300,000 girls. Girls Who Code is focusing on ensuring girls from underrepresented groups have access to quality STEM programs. The groups is expanding into creating curriculum and advocating policy changes to further their goals.

Books

Saujani is a New York Times bestselling author. She is also the host of the Brave, Not Perfect podcast.

  • Brave, Not Perfect, Saujani's most recent work, is inspired by her Ted Talk about how to encourage girls to be brave, take risks, and learn from failure.
  • The New York Times bestseller,Girls Who Code, is a how-to guide for younger girls wanting to learn how to code.
  • Saujani's first book, Women Who Don't Wait in Line, addresses the lack of women in leadership positions and suggests ways to promote women into those positions.

Investments

Saujani is using her prominence in the world of technology education to invest in companies founded by underrepresented groups. Her website provides a full and updated list. Some notable companies that have received funding from Saujani range from wellness coaches to wastewater epidemiology to harassment training. If it is innovative, Saujani is interested.
